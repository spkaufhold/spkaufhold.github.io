Stephan P. Kaufhold

I am a Psychologist and Cognitive Scientist studying the social behavior and cognition of primates using observational, experimental, and computational methods. Currently, I am a PhD student in the Department of Cognitive Science at UC San Diego, where I work in the Comparative Cognition Laboratory led by Dr. Federico Rossano.

Research Interests

I study how primates (including macaques, gibbons, orangutans, and chimpanzees) make decisions, navigate social relationships, and resolve resource conflicts. Taking a comparative approach, my research aims to contribute to our understanding of the evolution of human cognition. I primarily collect data from sanctuary-living animals in semi-naturalistic environments using non-invasive observational, quasi-experimental, and experimental methods. Some of my current research questions are:

  • What determines whether conspecifics choose to cooperate or compete in a given interaction, particularly in relation to their social relationships, group-level cultures, and environmental context?
  • How does physical proximity shape primates' social interactions, including social tolerance, social learning, and cooperation?
  • In what ways does culture influence primate behavior and cognition?
  • What are the ultimate and proximate effects of pathogens and parasites on primate behavior and cognition?

Current Projects

For my dissertation, I investigate how primates resolve resource conflicts with conspecifics, e.g. through cooperation and competition. Across four empirical projects, I documented that their strategies are flexible and depend on their specific social relationships, environments, and interactions between relationships and environments. Currently, I am conducting several research projects exploring social tolerance, inhibitory control, pathogen sensitivity, and social learning in macaques at the Born Free USA Primate Sanctuary in Southern Texas. For example, I am collaborating with Jack Terwilliger to develop and implement custom computer vision algorithms that accurately track macaques' proximity and trajectories using real-world coordinates during social tolerance tests.

Background

Before starting my PhD, I earned both a Bachelor's and Master's of Science in Psychology at the University of Innsbruck in Austria. During this time, I also interned and worked at the Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Psychology in Leipzig, Germany. During my PhD, I also completed a Specialization in Anthropogeny at the Center for Academic Research & Training in Anthropogeny (CARTA).
